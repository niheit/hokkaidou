excursions. This file is made based on my excursion notes at the time. 
(by T. Nihei)

Hokkaido --> Hokkaidou. This is because this file is for oral 
explanation (Nihei lab 2025). #1 #2

Sorachi [so-ra-chi] [\u7A7A\u77E5] is a sub-prefecture (``sougou-
shinkou-kyouku'') in central Hokkaidou (``dou-ou''). The area 
corresponds to the middle reaches of the Ishikari River. It is an 
inland sub-prefecture.
 	The name ``Sorachi'' comes from ``so-rapci-pet'' in Ainu 
language. It means ``waterfall, fall many, river.'' It signified the 
waterfall of the Sorachi River. #1
	The climate is of the inland type. Summers are warmer and 
winters are colder than the average of Hokkaidou. The area is located 
in the west of the mountains, therefore, it snows a lot. (In winter, 
wet winds blow from the northwest).
	According to the Koeppen's climate classification, the area is 
classified as Dfb (humid continental, warm summer). In the case of 
Iwamizawa City, the mean annual temperature is 7.6 degrees Celsius 
(average from 1981 to 2010), the mean annual precipitation is 1,163 
millimeters, and the mean annual snowfall is 753 centimeters. #2
 	In 1897 (Meiji 30), Sorachi sub-prefecture (Sorachi-shichou 
[\u7A7A\u77E5\u652F\u5E81]) was established. It covered Sorachi county 
(Sorachi-gun [\u7A7A\u77E5\u90E1]) in Ishikari-no-kuni (Ishikari 
country) [\u77F3\u72E9\u56FD]. (See Ishikari sub-prefecture for 
Ishikari country.)
	In 1899 (Meiji 32), Furano village was moved to Kamikawa sub-
prefecture. (Furano village covered the area what is now Kami-furano 
Village, Naka-furano Village, Furano City, and Minami-furano Town). In 
2010, Sorachi General Sub-prefectural Bureau (Sorachi-sougou-shinkou-
kyoku) was established.
	These are municipalities in Sorachi sub-prefecture; (1) 
Iwamizawa City 90,145 (population in 2010), (2) Takikawa City 43,170, 
(3) Bibai City 26,034, (4) Fukagawa City 23,709, (5) Sunagawa City 
19,056, (6) Ashibetsu City 16,628, (7) Kuriyama Town 13,340, 
	(continued) --> (8) Akabira City 12,637, (9) Naganuma Town 
11,691, (10) Yuubari City 10,922, (11) Mikasa City 10,221, (12) Nanporo 
Town 8,778, (13) Shin-totsukawa Town 7,249, (14) Naie Town 6,194, (15) 
Yuni Town 5,896, (16) Tsukigata Town 4,859, (17) Utashinai City 4,387, 
	(continued) --> (18) Kami-sunagawa Town 4,086, (19) Numata Town 
3,612, (20) Moseushi Town 3,462, (21) Uryuu Town 3,049, (22) 
Chippubetsu Town 2,730, (23) Urausu Town 2,206, and (24) Hokuryuu Town 
2,193.
 	The sub-prefecture bureau is located in Iwamizawa City. There 
are many cities in this sub-prefecture. They prospered due to coal 
mines in the past.

#1  The origin of the word ``Sorachi (waterfall, fall many, river)'' is 
the same as Takikawa City in Sorachi sub-prefecture. The name 
``Takikawa'' means ``waterfall, river'' in Japanese. The waterfall is 
the Sorachi Great Falls (Sorachi-oo-taki) in Ashibetsu City, Sorachi 
sub-prefecture. 

#2  In the case of Tokyo (Chiyoda Ward; the representative data of 
Japan), the mean annual temperature is 15.4 degrees Celsius (average 
from 1981 to 2010) and the mean annual rainfall is 1,529 millimeters. 
The climate of Tokyo is classified as Cfa (humid subtropical) by 
Koeppen's climate classification.
	As for the coldest month, the mean temperature of Tokyo is 5.2 
degrees Celsius in January. The mean temperature of Iwamizawa is -5.5 
degrees Celsius in January. As for the hottest month, the mean 
temperature of Tokyo is 26.4 degrees Celsius in August. The mean 
temperature of Iwamizawa is 21.3 degrees Celsius in August.
	In Hokkadou, it gets cold after Obon (15th August). In Tokyo, 
it finally gets cooler after the equinox (23rd September). (Cold and 
cool are feelings.)

|  MAP 5 - NO. 1  |
 -----------------
AREA 81 KM2 (2010), FOREST 1, CULTIVATED 55; COUNTY = SORACHI [\u7A7A
\u77E5]; POP 8,778 (2010), 5,665 (1990); COUNTRY SIGN = POPLAR & RIVER.
 
Nanporo [na-n-po-ro] [\u5357\u5E4C] is a town in Sorachi sub-
prefecture, central Hokkaidou (``dou-ou''). The area is located in the 
south of the Ishikari Plain. It is a small town. It is an inland town.
	The topography is flat. The area is surrounded by the Yuubari 
River to the north, the Kyuu-yuubari River (old Yuubari River) to the 
southeast, and the Chitose River to the west.
 	The name ``Nanporo'' means ``south, Horomui'' in Japanese. The 
name ``Horomui'' comes from ``poro-moy'' in Ainu language. It means 
``large, bend.'' The original ``Horomui'' is located in Iwamizawa City, 
where the Ishikari River bent. #1
	In 1886 (Meiji 19), ten families immigrated from Niigata 
Prefecture. In 1892 (Meiji 25), land surveying of Horomui wilderness 
(Horomui-guen-ya) was finished. A group of eighty families from Miyagui 
Prefecture (Miyagui-dantai) and a group of fifty families from Mie 
Prefecture (Mie-dantai) immigrated.
	In 1895 (Meiji 28), the administrative office (``kochou-
yakuba'') of Horomui village was established. It was separated from 
Kurisawa village (now Iwamizawa City). In 1909 (Meiji 42), Horomui 
[\u5E4C\u5411] Village (2nd-class municipality) was established. #2
	In 1962 (Shouwa 37), Minami-horo [\u5357\u5E4C] Town was 
established. The name was changed from Horomui to Minami-horo. In 1968 
(Shouwa 43), the pronunciation of the Kanji characters was changed 
from Minami-horo to Nanporo. #3
 	The town hall of Nanporo is located near the Kyuu-yuubari River 
(old Yuubari River). National Route 337 (Douou-kokudou) runs. The 
national highway bends as a crank along Colonial Rectangular Land 
Division (Shokuminchi-kukaku). (See Shin-totsukawa Town in Sorachi sub-
prefecture for Colonial Rectangular Land Division.)
 	A straight road that cuts through Colonial Rectangular 
Division is the former site of the Yuubari Railway. It connected 
Yuubari Station in Yuubari City and Nopporo Station in Ebetsu City. 
(Nopporo Station is on the Hakodate Main Line.)
	The trains shipped coal from the Yuubari mountains and 
agricultural products from Nanporo. There were Nanporo Station and 
Bansui Station in Nanporo Town. #4
	Nanporo is within a commuting zone of Sapporo City. The 
distance is 35 kilometers and the time is 41 minutes by car from the 
town hall of Nanporo to Sapporo Station (by an internet route search in 
2019). The population increased in the 1990s.
	Distinctive industry in Nanporo is agriculture on paddy fields.
STATISTICS OF AGRICULTURE = paddy rice 2,343 hectares in 2015, wheat 
1,678, beans (soybeans 452, adzuki beans 104, other beans 6), sugar 
beets 109, green onions 60, cabbage 55, broccoli 40, buckwheat 37, 
	(continued) --> fodder 21, onions 17, pasture 11, tomatoes 8, 
watermelons 7, potatoes 5, flowers 4, radishes 1, Chinese cabbages 1, 
other vegetables 71+X (pumpkins, sweet corn), and other grains 55; 
dairy cows X (concealed) head in 2015.
	As for sightseeing, Nanporo Hot Spring opened in 1993. The 
restaurant of the hot spring serves cabbage mega-tendon (cabbage large 
tempura bowl). Cabbage is a local specialty. #5
  	The area is surrounded by rivers. In the past, floods were 
frequent. From the Meiji period to the Shouwa period, flood control 
works were undertaken and farmland expanded. The downstrem of the 
Yuubari River is an artificial drainage. That was dug towards a 
chimney in Ebetsu. #6 #7

#1  The bend of the Ishikari River became an ox-bow lake. (``Ox-bow 
lake'' is called ``mikazuki-ko [crescent moon lake]'' in Japanese.)
The watercourse of the Ishikari River was straghtened to prevent flood 
damages.

#2  In Hokkaidou, there were 1st-class (``ikkyuu'') and 2nd-class 
(``nikyuu'') municipalities from 1899 to 1943. The Hokkaidou government 
subsidized 2nd-class municipalities. It was a pioneering period.

#3  In 1962, the name was changed from Horomui to Minami-horo, because 
there was the same name ``Horomui'' in Iwamizawa City. Horomui still 
exists in Iwamizawa City. There is Horomui Station on the Hakodate 
Main Line.
	In 1968, the name was changed from Minami-horo to Nanporo, 
because the pronunciation of the Kanji characters is difficult. 
``Nanporo'' is similar to Sapporo. Pronunciation of Kanji characters 
is difficult but interesting.

#4  As for the name ``Bansui [\u6669\u7FE0],'' according to the book 
``Minami-horo-chou-shi (Minami-horo Town History)'' published in 1962, 
the place was originally called Banguesou. In the past, the Panke-souka 
River and the Penke-souka River flew. The name ``Panke-souka'' changed 
to ``Bangesou,'' and then to ``Bansui.''
	The Panke-souka River and Penke-souka River were tributaries of 
the Ebetsu River. In Ainu language, ``panke'' means downstream, 
``penke'' means upstream, ``so'' means waterfall, and `ka'' means 
riverbank.
	The Ebetsu River was located downstream from the confluence of 
the old Yuubari River and the Chitose River. The Ebetsu River met the 
Ishikari River in Ebetsu City. The Ebetsu River became the Chitose 
River after the completion of the new Yuubari River. River courses were 
altered to prevent flooding.

#5  Cabbage and lettuce are leaf vegetables (you-sai [\u8449\u83DC]). 
Both are Western vegetables (you-sai [\u6D0B\u83DC]). The planted area 
of cabbage in Hokkaidou is 1,385 hectares (by 2015 Census of 
Agriculture). The planted area of lettuce in Hokkaidou is 601 hectares.
	The largest cabbage producing municipality in Hokkaidou is 
Wassamu Town (147 hectares). The largest lettuce producing municipality 
is Date city (75 hectares).
	Cabbage has a longer history than lettuce in the country. 
Cabbages has been cultivated since the Meiji period (150 years ago). 
Before World War II, cabbages were cultivated mainly in Hokkaidou and 
the Touhoku region. They grow well in cool temperatures.
	Cabbage is eaten raw or boiled. Lettuce is eaten raw. Lettuce 
cultivation spread after the 1970s. Lettuce tastes bitter and the 
taste pairs will with meat. My mother liked cabbage than lettuce. She 
boiled cabbage. In the past, raw vegetables could have worms 
(nematodes).
	Lettuce is a member of chrysanthemum family (``Asteraceae''). 
Cabbage is a member of rape family (``Brassicaceae''). The rape family 
plants produce cross-shaped flowers (juuji-bana), although cabbage 
rarely flowers.
	Radish is a member of rape family as well as cabbage. Radish 
flowers in unkempt vegetable gardens. Radish and cabbage become 
inedible once they flower. The status is called tou-ga-tatsu and means 
that the flower stalk stands or it becomes old.

#6  The downstream of the Yuubari River is an artificial drainage. It 
was constructed between 1922 and 1936. Today, the Yuubari River joins 
the Ishikari River. The Kyuu-yuubari River (old Yuubari River) is 
narrow and meandering and joins the Chitose River. The topography is 
flat. 
	Peat covered vast areas and the land was poorly drained. Once 
a major flood occurred, it took 10 days for the water to recede. Floods 
caused severe damage to crops.
	In the past, when floods started, local residents gathered on 
both sides of the river. They watched the water. When the embankment 
broke, the residents on the other side shouted ``banzai.'' (``banzai'' 
means ``ten thousand years!'' in Japanese. It signifies ``cheers!'') 
The Banzai-embankment existed 100 years ago.

#7  Motoji Hobara was the engineer who worked for the Hokkaidou 
Government after graduating from Tokyo Imperial University (now the 
University of Tokyo). He led the flood control projects of the Yuubari 
River from 1910 (Meiji 43). (Motoji Hobara --> http://ja.wikipedia.org/
wiki/%E4%BF%9D%E5%8E%9F%E5%85%83%E4%BA%8C)
	One day, he climbed a hill in Kuriyama. He saw a tall chimney 
of a paper mill in Ebetsu and thought, ``If we build a straight 
drainage to the Ishikari River, the watercourse of the Yuubari River 
will be shorten from 10.5 ri to 3 ri. The chimney is the landmark.'' 
(One ``ri'' is four kilometers in the Meiji period.)
	Due to the poor economic condition of the nation, the 
construction of watercourse was postponed until 1922 (Taishou 11). 
However, the construction did not progressed. Motoji Hobara and local 
residents submitted petitions to the national government. In 1934 
(Shouwa 9), the dredger ``Shouwa-gou'' was transferred from the 
Toyohira River to the Yuubari River. Then, the construction progressed.
	In 1936 (Shouwa 11), amidst the cheers of local residents, the 
new watercourse of the Yuubari River opened. That is the downstream of 
the current Yuubari River. Then, Motoji Hobara retired the Hokkaidou 
Government. He dedicated his life to the Yuurabri River. (by the book 
``Yuubari-gawa-chisui-shi-ryaku [Yuubari River water control history 
brief]'' published by Nanporo Town in 1994.)
	At Yoshitsune Shrine (now Nanporo Shrine), Flood Control 
Thanksgiving Festival (Chisui-kansha-sai) is held on July 1st. There is 
a statue of Motoji Hobara in the shrine. July 1st was the holiday of 
Nanporo Town. In neighboring Naganuma Town, Water Festival (Mizu-
matsuri) is held on July 2nd at Naganuma Shrine.

Naganuma [na-ga-nu-ma] [\u9577\u6CBC] is a town in Sorachi sub-
prefecture, central Hokkaidou (``dou-ou''). It is an inland town. The 
Ishikari Plain occupies most of the area. The topography is flat.
	The Chitose River, the Kyuu-yuubari River (old Yuubari River), 
and the Maoi [\u99AC\u8FFD] Hills make borders with other 
municipalities. #1 #2
	The name ``Naga-numa'' means ``long, pond'' in Japanese. It is 
a translation of ``tanne-to'' in Ainu language, which means ``long, 
pond.'' There were many ox-bow lakes. They were reclaimed. (``Ox-bow 
lake'' is called ``mikazuki-ko'' in Japanese, which means ``crescent 
moon lake.'')
	In 1887 (Meiji 20), Japanese immigration started. In 1892 
(Meiji 25), the name ``Naganuma'' was decided. In 1893 (Meiji 26), 
Colonial Rectangular Land Division (Shokumin-chi-kukaku) were set in 
Naganuma, and many immigrants moved there. (See Shin-totsukawa Town 
in Sorachi sub-prefecture for Colonial Rectangular Land Division.) #3
	In 1895 (Meiji 28), the administrative office (``kochou-
yakuba'') of Naganuma village was established. It was separated from 
Yuni village. In 1902 (Meiji 35), Naganuma Village (2nd-class 
municipality) was established.
	In 1907 (Meiji 40), Naganuma Village (1st-class municipality) 
was established. In 1952 (Shouwa 27), Naganuma Town was established. 
(See the note of Nanporo Town for 1st-class and 2nd-class 
municipalities.)
	The town hall of Naganuma is located along the Maoi Canal 
(Maoi-unga), which is a tributary of the Kyuu-yuubari River. National 
Route 337 (Douou-kokudou) runs. In the past, the Yuubari Railroad ran. 
It was operated by Hokutan (Hokkaidou Colliery and Steamship).
 	On the former Yuubri Railroad, there were Futaba Station, Kita-
naganuma Station, and Chuuou-noushi-mae Station. Kita-naganuma Station 
was located in Kita-shigai-chi (north built-up area). ``Chuuou-noushi-
mae'' means ``in front of Central Agricultural Experiment Station.''
	Distinctive industries in Naganuma are agriculture and rural 
tourism (green tourism). STATISTICS OF AGRICULTURE = wheat 2,752 
hectares in 2015, beans (soybeans 2,449, adzuki beans 38, other beans

fields spread. In 1966, the head office of Hokkaidou Agricultural 
Experiment Station was moved from Sapporo to Naganuma. In 1987, 
Naganuma Hot Spring opened. #4
  	Naganuma is near to Sapporo City and Chitose City. The 
population is maintained. Rural tourism thrives. #5

Yuni [yu-ni] [\u7531\u4EC1] is a town in Sorachi sub-prefecture, 
central Hokkaidou (``dou-ou''). It is an inland town. The area includes 
the Maoi Hills to the west and the Yuubari River to the east.
 	The name ``Yuni'' comes from  ``yu-un-i'' in Ainu language. It 
means ``hot spring, exist, place.'' Or, it comes from ``i-un-i'' in 
Ainu language. It means ``snake, exist, place.'' Both signified the 
Yuni River. There was a hot spring along the river.
	In 1891 (Meiji 24), a group of 119 people from Kumamoto 
Prefecture (Kumamoto-dantai) immigrated. In 1892 (Meiji 25), the 
administrative office (``kochou-yakuba'') of Yuni village was 
established. 
	In the same year, The Hokkaidou Colliery Railway opened (now 
the Muroran Main Line). Yuni Station became a transportation hub for 
neighboring settlements.
	In 1893 (Meiji 26), the administrative office of Yuni and 
other three villages was established. It covered Yuni village, Kadota 
village (now Kuriyama Town), Noborikawa village (now Yuubari City), and 
Naganuma village (now Naganuma Town).
	In 1895 (Meiji 28), the administrative office of Naganuma 
village was separated. In 1897 (Meiji 30), the administrative office of 
Noborikawa was separated. In 1900 (Meiji 33), the administrative office 
of Kakuta was separated.
 	In 1902 (Meiji 35), Yuni Village (2nd-class municipality) was 
established. In 1919 (Taishou 8), Yuni Village (1st-class municipality)
was established. In 1950 (Shouwa 25), Yuni Town was established. (See 
the note of Nanporo Town for 1st-class and 2nd-class municipalities.)
	The town hall of Yuni is located to the north. Near the town 
hall, the Yuni River meets the Yuubari River. National Route 234 (Yuni-
kokudou) and the Muroran Main Line pass three built-up areas; Yuni, 
Furusan, and Mikawa. #1
 	National Route 274 (Mikawa-kokudou) and the Sekishou Line pass 
through the south of the town. They run along Ryuusen-kyou Gorge on the 
Yuubari River. The gorge was created by the erosion of the Yuubari 
River and the uplift of the Yuubari Mountains.
	Distinctive industries in Yuni are agriculture and sightseeing.

	The road was built by Yamagata-dantai (an immigrant group from

 	Japan is a mountainous country. Roads curve with topography. 
Straight roads are unusual. Newly developed places have straight roads. 
For example, (1) Hokkaidou, which was developed mainly after the Meiji 
period, and (2) Shinden on Musashino Plateau, which was developed after 
the mid Edo era. (``Shinden'' means ``new, field'' in Japanese.)

Traditional villages in Honshuu island still have narrow roads if the 
soil is fertile.

Iwamizawa [i-wa-mi-za-wa] [\u5CA9\u898B\u6CA2] is a city in Sorachi 
sub-prefecture, central Hokkaidou (``dou-ou''). The area extends from 
the east bank of the Ishikari River to the Yuubari Mountains. The 
Ikushunbetsu River, the Horomui River, and the Kyuu-bibai River flow.
 	The name ``Iwamizawa'' comes from ``yuami-sawa [\u6D74\u6FA4]'' 
in Japanese. It means ``bathe, stream.'' In pioneering years, there was 
an inn for road construction. Workers could bathe there. The inn was 
located along the Ikushunbetsu River.
 	In 1882 (Meiji 15), Iwamizawa Station on the Horonai Line (now 
part of the Hakodate Main Line) opened. In 1884 (Meiji 17), 277 
families, 1,503 people, immigrated. They came mainly from Yamaguchi 
Prefecture and Tottori Prefecture. The administrative office (``kochou-
yakuba'') of Iwamizawa [\u5CA9\u898B\u6FA4] village was established.
	In 1892 (Meiji 25), the administrative office of Kurisawa 
[\u6817\u6FA4] village was established. It was separated from Iwamizawa 
village. The Hokkaidou Colliery Railway (now the Muroran Main Line) 
opened.
  	In 1900 (Meiji 33), Iwamizawa Village (1st-class municipality) 
was established with a population of 10,284 (by Iwamizawa City). The 
administrative office of Kitamura [\u5317\u6751] village was 
established. It was separated from Iwamizawa. (See the note of Nanporo 
Town for 1st-class and 2nd-class municipalities.)
	In 1906 (Meiji 39), Iwamizawa Town was established with a 
population of 19,812. Kurisawa Village (2nd-class municipalities) was 
established. In 1919 (Taishou 8), Kitamura Village (2nd-class 
municipality) was established. 
	In 1943 (Shouwa 18), Iwamizawa [\u5CA9\u898B\u6CA2] City 
was established with a population of 35,348. In 2006, Iwamizawa City 
merged Kurisawa Town and Kitamura Village. #1 #2
 	The city hall of Iwamizawa and Sorachi sub-prefecture bureau 
are located between the Ikushunbetsu River and the Horomui River. The 
former Iwamizawa Shrine was located along the Ikushunbetsu River. That 
is the original location of the settlement.
	The Hakodate Main Line and National Route 12 (Chuuou-kokudou) 
run. The Muroran Main Line and National Route 234 (Yuni-kokudou) 
terminate near the city hall.
	Iwamizawa was developed as a junction of railways in the Meiji 
period. The built-up area was known as ``Haikara (high collar),'' which 
means modern. Gas lamps illuminated in Iwamizawa for the first time 
in Hokkaidou. #3 #4 #5
	Distinctive industries in Iwamizawa are manufacturing, 
agriculture, commerce, and sightseeing. STATISTICS OF AGRICULTURE =

	As for sightseeing, a large Ferris wheel can be seen from the

	The Horonai Line was the third railway opened in the country.

Horonai Line.)
	The Hokkaidou Colliery Railway connected the stations of 
Muroran, Horobetsu, Noboribetsu, Shiraoi, Tomakomai, Oiwake, Yuni, and 
Iwamizawa. Those railroads were built for coal transportation. The 
railroads in Honshuu island were built for passengers from ports.
	For the opening of railroads in Hokkaidou, American advisors

Kuriyama [ku-ri-ya-ma] [\u6817\u5C71] is a town in Sorachi sub-
prefecture, central Hokkaiodu (``dou-ou''). The area extends from the 
east bank of the Yuubari River to the Yuubari mountains. It is an 
inland town.
	The name ``Kuriyama'' is Japanese. ``Kuri, yama'' means 
``chestnut, mountain.'' It is a translation of ``yam-ni-us-i'' in Ainu 
language, which means ``chestnut, tree, many, place.'' The 
municipalities of Kuriayama, Kurisawa (now Iwamizawa), and Ebetsu are 
the northern limit of natural chestnut trees. #1
 	In 1888 (Meiji 21), the former samurais from Kakuda Castle in 
the Sendai Domain immigrated. They were seven families, twenty four 
people. In 1892 (Meiji 25), the Hokkaidou Colliery Railway (now the 
Muroran Main Line) opened. In 1893 (Meiji 26), Kuriyama Station opened.
	In 1900 (Meiji 33), the administrative office (``kochou-
yakuba'') of Kakuta [\u89D2\u7530] village was established. (The 
pronunciation is Kakuta not Kakuda.) It was separated from Yuni 
village. In 1902 (Meiji 35), Kakuta Village (2nd-class municipality) 
was established. (See the note of Nanporo Town for 1st-class and 2nd-
class municipalities.)
 	In 1907 (Meiji 40), Kakuta Village (1st-class municipality) was 
established. In 1949 (Shouwa 24), Kuriyama Town was established. It was 
renamed from Kakuta. In 1963 (Shouwa 38), the town hall was moved to 
Kuriayama district from Kakuta district.
	The town hall of Kuriyama is located at the confluence of the 
Yuubari River and the Uenbetsu [\u96E8\u7159\u5225] River. National 
Route 234 (Yuni-kokudou) and the Muroran Main Line pass. The old 
Kuriyama Tunnel on the Muroran Line can be seen from National Route 
234. #2 #3
	In the past, the Yuubari Railway ran. Trains transported coal 
and coal miners. It connected Nopporo in Ebetsu City, Minamihoro (now 
Nanporo Town), Kuriyama Town, and Yuubari City from 1926 to 1975.
	Kakuta Coal Mine operated from 1933 to 1970. The population of 
Kuriyama Tow peaked in 1960 at 23,003 (by Population Census). (See 
Yuubari City in Sorachi sub-prefecture for coal mining.)
	National Route 274 (Mikawa-kokudou) and the Sekishou Line pass 
to the south. There is no station on the Sekisou Line. The Kawabata Dam 
over the Yuubari River stands out from the Dou-tou Expressway. It is 
located on the border between Kuriyama and Yuni towns. (See Yuni Town 
in Sorachi sub-prefecture for the dam.)
 	Distinctive industries in Kuriyama are agriculture, 
manufacturing, and sightseeing. STATISTICS OF AGRICULTURE = paddy rice

15, other beans 2), potatoes 214, fodder 52, pasture 44, sugar beets

	In 1895 (Meiji 28), the first agricultural irrigation 
association (suiri-kumiai) was established. It became a model for paddy 
rice cultivation in Hokkaidou. As for manufacturing, shipments of 
wooden products and earthenware are large.
	As for sightseeing, Kobayashi brewery (Kobayashi-shuzou) is 
located along the Yuubari River. The old buildings including rice wine
warehouses are designated as Tangible Cultural Properties (Yuukei-
bunkazai). Neighboring farmers cultivate brewery rice in summer and 
work for the brewery to make rice wine in winter. #4

Yuubari [yu-u-ba-ri] [\u5915\u5F35] is a city in Sorachi sub-
prefecture, central Hokkadou (``dou-ou''). It is located in the upper 
reaches of the Yuubari River. It is a city in the Yuubari Mountains. 
Forests occupy 86 percent of the area. #1 
 	The name ``Yuubari'' comes from ``yu-paro'' in Ainu language. 
It means ``hot spring, river mouth.'' Or, it comes from ``i-par'' in 
Ainu language. It means ``it, mouth.'' Both signify the mouth of the 
Yuubari River. In the past, Yuubari River met the Chitose River. Low 
temperature hot spring (kou-sen) flowed into the Yuubari River. #2
 	In 1888 (Meiji 21), a coal strata was discovered along the 
Shihoro-kabetsu River, which is a tributary of the Yuubari River. In 
1892 (Meii 25), Hokkaidou Colliery Co. (Hokutan) started coal mining. 
The Hokkaidou Colliery Railway opened between Yuubari and Oiwake.
 	In the same year, the Hokkaidou Colliery Railway opened 
between Iwamizawa, Oiwake, and Muroran. The company started 
transporting coal from the Yuubari mountains to the Port of Muroran and 
to the Port of Otaru. (See Iwaizawa City in Sorachi sub-prefecture for 
stations on Hokkaidou Colliery Railway.)
	In 1897 (Meiji 30), the administrative office (``kochou-
yakuba'') of Noborikawa [\u767B\u5DDD] village was established. It was 
separated from Yuni village. #3
	In 1906 (Meiji 39), Noborikawa Village (2nd-class municipality) 
was estabised. In 1918 (Taishou 7), the name was changed from Noborkawa 
to Yuubari. In 1919 (Taisou 8), Yuubari Town (1st-class municipality) 
was established. In 1943 (Shouwa 18), Yuubari City was established. 
(See the note of Nanporo Town for 1st-class and 2nd-class 
municipalities.)
	The city hall of Yuubari is located along the Shihoro-kabetsu 
River. It is located in a narrow valley. Yuubari Station near the 
city hall opened between 1892 and 2019. After the opening of the 
Sekishou Line in 1981, it became a terminal for the Yuubari branch of 
the Sekishou Line.
	To the south, the Sekishou Line and National Route 274 
(Hobetsu-kokudou) run. (The owner of the Sekisou Line changed from 
JNR [Japanese National Railways] to JR [Japan Raiway] Hokkaidou in 
1987.) Near Shin-yuubari Station on the Sekishou Line, National Route 
452 (Yuubari-kokudou) branches off from National Route 274. #4
	National Route 452 runs along the Yuubari River. It passes Lake 
Shuuparo (dam lake) in Yuubari City, Katsurazwa Lake (dam lake) in 
Mikasa City, and reaches Ashibetsu City. It does not pass the city 
hall of Yuubari.
  	Yuubari was a city of coal mining. It flourished from the Meiji 
period to the Shouwa period. The population peaked at 107,972 in 1960 
(Shouwa 35) (by Population Census). Today, Yuubari is the third least 
populated city in the country. 
	(The least is Utashinai City [4,390 people in 2010] and the 
second least is Mikasa City [10,255]. Both are coal mining 
municipalities in Sorachi sub-prefecture.) 
	(For coal mining, see Kuriyama Town, Bibai City, Mikasa City, 
Naie Town, Ashibetsu City, Kami-sunagawa Town, Utashinai City, Akabira 
City, and Numata Town in Sorachi sub-prefecture. For coal 
transportation, see Otaru City in Shiribeshi sub-prefecture, Abira 
Town, Muroran City in Iburi sub-prefecture, Iwamizawa City, and 
Sunagawa City in Sorachi sub-prefecture.) #5 #6 #7 #8 #9 #10
 	Distinctive industries in Yuubari are sightseeing and 
agriculture. STATISTICS OF AGRICULTURE = Melons 248 hectares in 2015,
pasture 38, paddy rice 23, yams 8, fodder 7, tomatoes 5, beans 4, 
watermelons 1, and other vegetables 17; dairy cow - (zero) in 2015.
	As for agriculture, arable land stretches in valleys. The 
area is small. Horticulture thrieves. Yuubari Melon is famous. #11 #12
	After the closure of coal mines, Yuubari changed to a tourist 
city. That was a policy of Tetsuji Nakada, who served as mayor from 
1979 to 2003. Tourist attractions were establised such as the Coal 
Museum, Coal Historical Village (Sekitan-no-rekishi-mura), Melon 
Castle, Yuubari Robot Museum, and Mt. Reisui Ski Resort, and Yuubari 
Film Festival.
	Investment in tourism sectors increased, the population 
decreased, and Yuubari City was designated as a municipality for 
financial reconstruction (zasei-saiken-dantai) in 2007. Meron Castle 
was abandoned. The Coal Historical Village and the Yuubari Robot 
Museum were closed. #13 #14

#9  The coal of Hokkaidou is originated mainly from meta-sequoia 
deposits. Meta-sequoia is a deciduous and coniferous tree. It can be 
seen many streets in the country, including the Tama Hills where I 
live now (Nihei lab 2023b).
	Meta-sequoia trees grew densely in Hokkaidou during Tertiary 
period of Cenozoic era (2.5-65 million years ago). The coal is new. In 
the USA and Europe, coal is mined from older strata, which were created 
in the Carboniferous period of Paleozoic era (300-350 million years 
ago).
	* * *
	Coal is used for industrial purposes. Large factories including
power plants, paper mills, and steel mills, use coal and coke. (Coke is 
made from coal and use in steel mills. Coke furnaces generate high 
temperatures.)
        For domestic purposes, coal stoves were used until the 1970s 
in Sapporo. I lived in an old teacher's apartment, which was built in 
the 1960s. Each room equipped a coal storage. Small pieces of coal were
found in the apartment's garden.
	From the 1970s, kerosene stoves spread. From the 2000s, air 
conditioners spread. Japan is a maritime country. Tankers import oil 
and colliers import coal.

#10  A house in ``The Yellow Handkerchief (1977 film)'' is preserved 
in Yuubari. It is a typical coal miner's house. Coal miner's houses and 
coal miner's apartments were the symbol of ``Ichizan-ikka.'' It means 
``one mountain, one family.'' 

#11  Yuubari Melon has orange pulp. The color was unique. Melons 
generally had green pulp. When I traveled Hokkaidou in the 1990s, I 
stopped at a melon shop along a road.
	The seller, a middle-aged woman, sold two types of melons, 
green and orange. She said, ``Green pulp is sweeter.'' My friend said, 
``Orange is a cross-bred with pumpkin.'' I bought green one. (The 
orange one was not Yuubari Melon.)
	* * *
 	As for Yuubari Melon, farmers started studying how to cultivate 
melons in the 1950s. They managed to get the melon seeds that could be 
used for cross-breeding. Those were original seeds. The farmers 
experimented cross-breeding. The first Yuubari Melon was sold in 1961. 
	(Today, melon seeds and seedlings are sold by companies such as 
Sakata Seed, which sells melon varieties of Andesu and Prince. The name 
``Andesu'' comes from Anshin-desu in Japanese. It means ``relief.'' It 
signifies ``relief for cultivation, relief for selling, and relief for 
eating.'')
	* * *
	Farmers in Yuubari also adopted a unique salling method. They
distribute only catalogues at department stores. Yuubari Melon could 
not be bought at general grocery stores.
 	In 1973, farmers registered the trademark of Yuubari Melon. In 
1979, they registered the design of Yuubari Melon sticker, which is 
put on the melons. In 1993, they registered the name ``Yuubari Melon.'' 
The registration was difficult, because ``Yuubari'' is a common name 
(place name) and ``melon'' is also a common name.
 	In 2006, 154 farmers cultivate melons in Yuubari. The harvest 
amounts 5,000 tons. Sales total 3,000,000,000 yen. The peak of the 
harvest is from late June to early August. Melon farmers cultivate 
nothing after melons in the year. They keep honey bees for polination.
	(In the case of the Kantou region, melon farmers cultivate 
tomatoes after melons. They keep honey bees for melons and bumblebees 
for tomatoes [by Nihei 2002 ``Technological innovations in the 
continuation of greenhouse horticulture in Asahi City.'' http://
www.researchgate.net/publication/270038030].) (See Biratori Town in 
Hidaka sub-prefecture for bees.)
 	The first melons are harvested in May in Yuubari. Those were 
sown in January and cultivated in hothouses. The prices of the first 
auction for two Yuubari Melons were 800,000 yen in 2006, 2,000,000 
yen in 2007, and 1,000,000 yen in 2011. They were the celebration 
prices (go-shuugui-souba). They were also advertising.
	These are the conditions for making Yubari Melon. The soil of 
Yuubari is composed of volcanic ash. Soil temperature rises in summer 
both in upland fields and paddy fields. Drainage is good. (Melons has 
been cultivated in paddy fields after the enactment of the rice 
reduction policy [``guentan-seisaku''].)
	Yuubari is surrounded by mountainous and the temperature 
decreases at night. Melons become sweeter. (At cool nights, crops 
sleep without consuming the sugars they produced during the day.)

Tsukigata [tsu-ki-ga-ta] [\u6708\u5F62] is a town in Sorachi sub-
prefecture, central Hokkaidou (Dou-nan). The area is located on the 
west bank of the Ishikari River.
	The name ``Tsukigata'' comes from Kiyoshi Tsukigata, who was 
the first warden of Kabato Prison (Kabato-shuuchi-kan). He was the 
former samurai from the Fukuoka Domain.
	The name ``Kabato'' comes from ``kapato'' in Ainu language. It 
means ``yellow pond lily (`kouhone' in Japanese).'' Ainu people ate 
the roots of yellow pond lily.
 	In 1881 (Meiji 14), Kabato Prison was established. The 
administrative office (``kochou-yakuba'') of Tsukigata was established. 
In 1882 (Meiji 15), the county hall (``gun-yakusho'') of Kabato was 
established. It covered Uryuu county and Kamikawa county. #1 #2
 	They were the first administrative office and the first county 
hall in the middle and upper reaches of the Ishikari River. (Today, 
Uryuu county is located in the north of the Ishikari Plain. Kamikawa 
county is located in the Kamikawa Basin, which includes Asahikawa 
City). 
 	In 1906 (Meiji 39), Tsukigata Village (2nd-class municipality) 
was established. In 1953 (Shouwa 28), Tsukigata Town was established. 
(See the note of Nanporo Town for 2nd-class municipality.) 
	The town hall of Tsukigata is located near the Subetsu [\u9808
\u90E8\u90FD] River. National Route 275 and Sasshou line pass. 
Tsukigata Hot Spring is located near the town hall. #3
 	Distinctive industries in Tsukigata are agriculture and 
sightseeing. STATISTICS OF AGRICULTURE = paddy rice 1,129 hectares in 
2015, wheat 334, beans (soybeans 292, adzuki beans 4), pasture 229, 
	(continued) --> flowers 22 (greenhouse), melons 18, buckwheat 
17, watermelons 14, tomatoes 10, potatoes 9, fodder 6, radishes 4, and 
other vegetables 55+X (pumpkins); dairy cows X (concealed) in 2015.
	Floriculture increased after the enactment of the rice 
reduction policy (``guentan-seisaku'') in the 1970s. Tsukigata became 
one of the largest flower producing municipalties in Hokkaidou.
	Floriculture thrives. The area of floriculture in 2005 counted 
72 hectares (open-field and hothouse), marking the largest in 
Hokkaidou. The main flowers are lilies, carnations, lisianthus 
(``Toruko-kikyou [Turky-kikyou]'' in Japanese), calla lilies, and 
delphinums. (In 2015, the area of floriculture is concealed.) 
	As for sightseeing, the old building of Kabato Prison is a 
tourist attraction. It opened as the Hokkaidou Prison Museum 
(Hokkaidou-gyoukei-shiryou-kan) in 1973. It became the Tsukigata-kabato 
Museum in 1996. It has been designated as Hokkaidou Heritage since 
2018.
	 The novel ``Akai-hito (red people)'' was written by Akira 
Yoshimura in 1977. It was based on real prisoners in Hokkaidou in the 
Meiji period, such as Inazuma-kozou (lightning boy). In connection with 
Hokkaidou University, Shinpachi Nagakura instructed swordmanship to the 
guarts of the Kabato Prison. #4 #5

#1  Kabato Prison was the first prison in Hokkaidou. It was a national 
prison in the Meiji period. The place Kabato was selected because of 
its good access by boat on the Ishikari River.
	There were other two potential places to built the national 
prison. One was Tokachi, however, it was a very remote place at the 
time. The other was around the foot of Mt. Youtei, however, it had 
already many houses. One of the purposes for making the prison in 
Hokkaidou was development.
	Another purpose for making the prison in Hokkaidou was to 
incarcerate political criminals. There were civil wars in the Meiji 
period; the Boshin War, Hagui Rebellion, Saga Rebellion, and the 
Satsuma War (Seinan-sensou). Defeated soldiers were political 
criminals.
	Four prisons were built in Hokkaidou in the Meiji period; (1) 
Kabato Prison (Kabato-shuuchi-kan), established in 1881 (Meiji 14), 
located in what is now Tsukigata Town, Sorachi sub-prefecture. 
	(continued) --> (2) Sorachi Prison (Sorachi-shuuchi-kan), 
established in 1882 (Meiji 15), located in what is now Mikasa City, 
Sorachi sub-prefecture. (3) Kushiro Prison (Kushiro-shuuchi-kan), 
established in 1885 (Meiji 18), located in what is now Shibecha Town, 
Kushiro sub-prefecture.
	(continued) --> (4) Abashiri branch of Kushiro Prison, 
established in 1890 (Meiji 23), located in what is now Abashiri City,
Okhotsk sub-prefecture. Prisoners cleared the wilderness and 
constructed roads.
	In 1919 (Taishou 8), Kabato Prison was moved to Asahikawa 
Ward (now Asahikawa City). Today, prisons are located in Sapporo City, 
Asahikawa City, Obihiro City, Kushiro City (Kushiro branch of Obihiro 
Prison), Abashiri City, Tsukigata Town, and Hakodate City (juvenile 
prison).
	The current Tsukigata Prison was established in 1983. Before 
the establishment, the Japanese government was searching for a site for 
a new prison to replace Nakano Prison in Tokyo. Tsukigata Town invited 
the new prison and was selected, because the town had a history of 
a penal system.
	(In Nakano Prison, Tokyo, the novelist Takiji Kitaji was 
incarcerated before the war. He wrote ``The Crab Cannery Ship.'' See 
Otaru City in Shiribesi sub-prefecture for the novelist.)
	I heard that the food of Tsukigata Prison is good. (In 
Hokkaidou, food is good.) Prisoners in Tsukigata produce tables and 
chairs, which called ``ashi-mono (things with legs).''
	The products were sold at a on-line shipping site of CAPIC 
(Correctional Association Prison Industry Cooperation). How about 
buying a wooden chair for your office?

#2  Prisoner roads (prisoner-built roads) in Hokkaidou were called 
``Shuujin-douro'' in Japanese. Prisoners constructed roads in the Meiji 
Meiji period. The first one opened between Kabato Prison and Sorachi 
Prison. (Kabato Prison was located in what is now Tsukigata Town and 
Sorachi Prison was located in what is now Mikasa City.)
	The first prisoner road was constructed between 1886 and 1890. 
The road traversed the Ishikari Plain, crossing the Ishikari River by 
boat. It is now called Kabato Road (Kabato-douro, part of Prefectural 
Route 275), which connects Tsukugata Town, Bibai City, and Mikasa City. 
The road crosses Tsukigata-oohashi Bridge over the Ishikari River,  
passes near Miyajima Pond. 
	The road is almost straight, although it bends slightly. The 
shape like the Japanese word ``he.'' The road detoured large ponds 
at the time. http://cyberjapandata.gsi.go.jp/#12/43.301196/141.753502/
	Logs were laid on the road in the Meiji period. They allow 
horse-drawn carriages to pass the Ishikari Plain. The logs were made 
from Japanese elm (Haru-nire or Akadamo). The trees were rampant in the 
plain in the Meiji period. Today, the straight road passes through 
extensive paddy fields. (See Yuni Town in Sorachi sub-prefecture for 
straight roads).
	These are major prisoner roads;
	(1) Kabato Road (see above); built by the prisoners of Kabato 
Prison and Soracih Prison; It can be seen clearly from Mt. Tappu in 
Mikasa City.
	(2) Kamikawa Road (Kamikawa-douro); built between 1887 and 
1889, by the prisoners of Kabato Prison and Sorachi Prison; connecting 
Ichikisiri (now Mikasa City) and Chuubetsu-but (now Asahikawa City); 
It is now part of National Route 12.
	(3) Teshio Road (Teshio-douro); built between 1887 and 1892, 
mainly by the prisoners of Kabato Prison; connecting Kabato Prison (now 
Tsukigata Town) in the Ishikari Plain and Mashike on the Sea of Japan 
side; It is now part of National Route 275 and part of Prefectural 
Route 94. (http://cyberjapandata.gsi.go.jp/#12/43.743817/141.688614/) 
	(4) Yuubari Road; built between 1889 and 1890, mainly by 
the prisoners of Sorachi Prison; connecting Iwamizawa, Kakuta (now 
Kuriyama Town), and Yuubari; It is an old road along the foot of the 
Yuubari Mountains. (See Kuriyama Town in Sorachi sub-prefecture.)
 	(5) Central Road (Chuuou-douro); built in 1891, mainly by the 
prisoners of Abashiri branch of Kushiro Prison; connecting Asahikawa 
and Abashiri; It is now part of National Route 39.
	Prisoner roads accompanied sacrifice. The dead were buried 
along the roads. The burial mounds are called Chain-mounds (kusari-
zuka). Chain shackles were tombs.
	Professor Chiaki Oguchi at University of Tsukuba described the 
situation as ``gradual death sentence (kanman-na-shikei).'' (For 
prisons and prisoner roads, see Kuriyama Town, Mikasa City in Sorachi 
sub-prefecture, Obihiro City in Tokachi sub-prefecture, Kitami City, 
Abashiri City in Okhotsk sub-prefecture, Shibecha Town, Teshikaga Town, 
and Shiranuka Town in Kushiro sub-prefecture.)

#3  ``Subetsu'' is an old place name. It comes from ``si-pet'' in Ainu 
language. It means ``large, river.'' The meaning is the same as 
Shibetsu City in Kamikawa sub-prefecture and Shibetsu Town in Nemuro 
sub-prefecture.
	The pronunciation ``shi-betsu (subetsu, or si-pet)'' in 
Japanese sounds like ``death, separate.'' The first Japanese immigrants 
used Kabato instead of Subetsu.
	Today, Kabato-sakai River flows on the border between Urausu 
Town and Shin-totsukawa Town, and the Kabato River flows in Shin-
totsukawa Town. Both are located north of Tsukigata Town.

#4   As for the novel ``Akai-hito (Red People),'' prisoners in 
Hokkaidou during the Meiji period wore red clothes. If prisoners 
escape, they could be found soon by the color.
	The novel is fiction but written based on real prisoners. These 
were unique prisoners; 
	(1) Keijirou Sakamoto; a burglar, murderer, rapist, and prison 
escapee. He was incarcerated in Kabato and other prisons. He escaped 
from Kabato Prison. He run away 48-ri (188 kilometers) per day. His 
nickname was lightning boy (Inazuma-kozou).
	(2) Torakichi Nishikawa; an attempted murderer, arson, and 
prison escapee. He was incarcerated in Kabato, Abashiri, Akita, and 
other prisons. He escaped from Akita Prison in Honshuu island. At the 
time, he stepped on a board with 15-centimeters' nails (to prevent 
prisoners from escaping).
	His nickname was Gosunkugi-no-torakichi (Torakichi of 15 
centimeters' nail]). He was also called as the king of prison breaks 
(datsugoku-ou). (See Hidaka Town in Hidaka sub-prefecture for a story 
of 15-centimeters' nail.)
	(3) Chouan Kumasaka; a counterfeiter (counterfeit banknote 
maker). He was an mysterious person. He was a painter, a medical 
doctor, and a teacher. He might have been falsely accused. He dead in 
Tsukigata Prison. His life was also depicted in the novel ``Fuun-na-
namae (unfortunate name)'' written by Seichou Matsumoto.
	(As for counterfeiter, the cartoon ``Lupin the Third'' depicts 
one. Lupin said `Your work is better than the real one' and the 
counterfeiter was disappointed. See Hamanaka Town in Kushiro sub-
prefecture for Lupin the Third.)
 
#5  Shinpachi Nagakura was the strongest swordsman in Shinsen-gumi.  
(Shinsen-gumi was the special forces of the Tokugawa Shogunage.) His 
special sword movement was ``Ryuuhi-ken (flying dragon sword).'' The 
sword moved from bottom to top.
	Shinpachi Nagakura fled the Matsumae Domain (now Matsumae Town 
in Oshima sub-prefecture). After the Meiji period, he lived in Otaru
and instructed swordsman ship in Kabato Prison and in Agriculture 
Collage of Touhoku Imperial University (now Hokkaidou University).
	There is a small monument near the main gate of Hokkaidou 
University, which read ``the place associated with Shinpachi 
Nagakura.'' (See Hakodate City in Oshima sub-prefecture for the 
swordsman.)

Bibai [bi-ba-i] [\u7F8E\u5504] is a city in Sorachi sub-prefecture,

pond.'' In 1891 (Meiji 24), Bibai Station on the Hokkaidou Colliery 
Railway (now the Hakodate Main Line) opened.
 	In 1891 and 1894 (Meij 24 and 27), the military settlers' 
colony, Bibai-heison, was established, and 160 families immigrated. In 
1895 (Meiji 28), the administrative office of Kainuma village was 
established. In 1906 (Meiji 39), Kainuma Village (2nd-class 
municipality) was established. (See the note of Nanporo Town for 1st-
class and 2nd-class municipalities.)
 	In 1909 (Meiji 42), Kainuma Village (1st-class municipality) 
was established. In 1925 (Taishou 14), Kainuma Town was established. In 
1926 (Taishou 15), the name changed from Kainuma to Bibai. In 1950 
(Shouwa 25), Bibai City was established.
	The city hall of Bibai is located where the Bibai River enters 
the Ishikari Plain. National Route 12 (chuuou-kokudou) and the 
Hakodate Main Line pass. There is a sign board on National Route 12 
that reads, ``The longest straight road in Japan, 29.2 kilometers.'' 
(See Yuni Town in Sorachi sub-prefecture for straight roads.)
	There were two large coal mines in the Yuubari Mountans. They 
were Mitsubishi Bibai Coal Mine and Hokuryou-garo Coal Mine (a 
Mitsubishi group company). The Bibai Line was operated by Mitsubishi 
and transported coal.
	To the south of the city, there was another large coal mine. 
It was Mitsui Bibai Coal Mine located on the foot of the Yuubari 
Mountains. (Mitsui and Mitsubishi were the former ``zaibatsu [huge 
conglomerate]''.) 
	The population of Bibai peaked in 1954 at 91,431 (by Bibai 
City). All of them were underground coal mines and closed in the 
1970s. (See Yuubari City in Sorachi sub-prefecture for coal mining.)

 	In 1879 (Meiji 12), Horonai Coal Mine opened. In 1882 (Meiji

Shin-totsukawa [shi-n-to-tsu-ka-wa] [\u65B0\u5341\u6D25\u5DDD] is a 
town in Sorachi sub-prefecture, central Hokkaidou (``dou-ou''). The 
area extends from the west bank (right bank) of the Ishikari River to 
the Mashike Mountains. Mt. Pin-ne-shiri (1,100 meters) stands out from 
The Ishikari Plain. #2
  	The name ``Shin-totsukawa'' is Japanese. It means ``New 
Totsukawa.'' The original Totsukawa Village is located in Nara 
Prefecture. (The name ``Totsukawa'' comes from the Totsukawa Rver 
flowing in Nara Prefecture.)
	In 1889 (Meiji 22), Colonial Rectangular Land Division 
(Shokuminchi-kukaku [\u6B96\u6C11\u5730\u533A\u753B]) was set in Tokku 
wilderness (Tokku-guen-ya [\u30C8\u30C3\u30AF\u539F\u91CE]). In the 
same year, a major flood disaster occurred along the Totsukawa River 
in Nara Prefecture.
	In 1890 (Meiji 23), 600 families, 2,489 people, who affected by 
the flood disaster, moved to Tokku wilderness. In the same year, the 
administrative office (``kochou-yakuba'') of Shin-totsukawa village 
was established. #3 #4 #5 #6 
	For the next ten years, there were no immigrants from other 
prefectures. It is a unique immigration history in Hokkaidou. In 1902 
(Meiji 35), Shin-totsukawa Village (2nd-class municipality) was 
established. In 1957 (Shouwa 32), Shin-totsukawa Town was established. 
(See the note of Nanporo Town for 1st-class and 2nd-class 
municipalities.)
	The town hall of Shin-totsukawa is located near the confluence 
of the Toppu River and the Ishikari River. National Route 275 (Sorachi-
kokudou) runs north on the west bank of the Ishikari River. #7
	National Route 451 (Shokan-kokudou) branches off from National 
Route 275, passes the Mashike Mountains, and reaches the former 
Hamamasu Village (now Ishikari City).
 	Shin-totsukawa Station is the terminal of the Sasshou Line. The 
name ``Sasshou'' comes from ``Satsu-shou,'' which signifies Sapporo and 
Numata. The railroad connected Sapporo City in Ishikari sub-prefecture 
and Numata Town in Sorachi sub-prefecture until 1972. #8
 	Distinctive industries in Shin-totsukawa are paddy rice 
cultivation and sightseeing. STATISTICS OF AGRICULTURE = paddy rice 
3,540 hectares in 2015, buckwheat 363, beans (soybeans 135, adzuki 
beans 4), wheat 135, pasture 84, onions 38, green onions 8, fodder 7, 
	(continued) --> tomatoes 5, melons 5, broccoli 4, orchards 1, 
and other vegetables 36+X; dairy cows X (concealed) head in 2015.
	In the past, rice paddies stretched upstream to valleys, 
including the Socchi-gawa [\u7DCF\u5BCC\u5730\u5DDD] River. Rice 
production exceeded the consumption, the rice reduction policy 
(``guentan-seisaku'') was enacted, and rice paddies in valleys shrunk. 
The land immigrants cleared returned forests.
 	As for sightseeing, the Pioneer Museum (Kaitaku-kinen-kan) 
is located near the town hall. Next to the museum is Izumo-taisha 
Shrine. Green Park Shin-totsukawa is located on the river terrace of 
the Ishikari River. The Shin-totsukawa Story Museum, a hot spring, and 
sports facilities are located on the park. #9 #10

#3  Totsukawa Village in Nara Prefecuture is the largest village in 
Japan (excluding towns and cities). The area occupies 672 square 
kilometers. (Tokyo 23 Wards are 622 square kilometers [62,200 
hectares].) It occupies one fifth of Nara Prefecture.

#4 The first Colonial Rectangular Land Division (Shokuminchi-kukaku) 
was set in Tokku wilderness (Tokku-guen-ya). The name ``Tokku'' means 
``protruding'' in Ainu language. The second Colonial Rectangular Land 
Division was set in Nae wilderness (Nae-guen-ya) in what is now Naie 
Town, Sorachi sub-prefecture.
	Colonial Rectangular Land Divisions were set only in Hokkaidou 
Prefecture. It was during the Meiji period. Colonial Rectangular Land 
Division is similar to Township in the USA. Both land divisions have 
straight roads stretching along rectangular plots. However, in 
Hokkaidou, the roads do not run in a north-south direction.
	A large plot (``dai-kukaku'') of Colonial Rectangular Land 
Division is 900-ken on a side. ``900-ken'' is equivalent to 15-chou or 
1,620 meters.) A middle plot (``chuu-kukaku'') is 300-ken on a side. 
``300-ken'' is equivalent to 5-chou or 545 meters.
 	In the cases of the Ishikari Plain and the Tokachi Plain, five 
families settled in the middle plot of Colonial Rectangular Land 
Division. The area of a middle plot is about 30 hectares (545 x 545 
meters = 297,025 square meters). Each settler owned six hectares of 
land and cultivated grains, beans, and vegetables.
	As for grain cultivation in the past, in the case of the 
Ishikari Plain, farmers cultivated millet (``awa''), glutinous proso 
millet (``ina-kibi''), and rice. In the case of the Tokachi plain, 
farmers cultivated millets. Farmers in both area also cultivated 
corn (maize), potatoes, and beans.
	Agricultural mechanization progressed in the 1970s. The number 
of farms decreased. The average farm size in 2010 is 14 hectares in the 
Ishikari Plain and 38 hectares in the Tokachi Plain (by Census of 
Agriculture). The average of Honshuu island is 1.6 hectares.

meters.
	A quarter section is a quarter mile on a side. A quarter mile

the land an immigrant could acquire in the western part of the USA (by

Sunagawa [su-na-ga-wa] [\u7802\u5DDD] is a city in Sorachi sub-
prefecture, central Hokkaidou (``dou-ou''). It is located in the east 
bank (left bank) of the Ishikari River. The Sorachi River meets the 
Ishikari River to the north.
	The name ``suna-gawa'' means ''sand, river'' in Japanese. It is 
a translation of ``ota-us-nay'' in Ainu language, which means ``sand, 
exist, river.'' It signifies the Penke-utashinai River and the Panke-
utashinai River. In Ainu language, ``penke'' means ``up'' and `
`panke'' means ``down.''
 	In 1887 (Meiji 20), a road opened between Ichikishiri (now 
Mikasa City) and Asahikawa. The road was constructed by about 500 
prisoners (See Tsukigata Town in Sorachi sub-prefecture for prisoners.) 
	Sunagawa became a hub of road and river transportation. In 
1890 (Meiji 23), Nae village was established in what is now Sunagawa 
City. The administrative office (``kochou-yakuba'') was co-operated in 
Takikawa village.
	In 1891 (Meiji 24), Sunagawa Station and Naie Station on the 
Hokkaidou Colliery Railway (now the Hakodate Main Line) opened. In 
1895 (Meiji 28), an administrative office was built in Nae village.
	In 1897 (Meii 30), Utashinai village was separated from Nae 
village. (Utashinai village covered the area what is now Utashinai 
City, Ashibetsu City, and Akabira City.) In 1902 (Meiji 35), Nae 
Village (2nd-class municipality) was established. (See the note of
Nanporo Town for 1st-class and 2nd-class municipalities.)
	In 1903 (Meiji 36), the name changed from Nae to Sunagawa. In 
1907 (Meiji 40), Sunagawa Village (1st-class municipality) was 
established. In 1923 (Taishou 12), Sunagawa Town was established.
	In 1944 (Shouwa 19), Naie Village was separated (now Naie 
Town). In 1949 (Shouwa 24), Kami-sunagawa Town was separated. In 1958 
(Shouwa 33), Sunagawa City was established.
	The city hall of Sunagawa is located between the Penke-
utashinai River and the Panke-utashinai River. National Route 12 
(Chuuou-kokudou) and the Hakodate Mein Line pass. The national highway 
is the longest straight road in Japan. (See Yuni Town in Sorachi sub-
prefecture for straight roads.)
	In the past, Sunagawa was a hub for coal transportation. The 
Utashinai Line and the Sunagawa Branch Line branched off from Sunagawa 
Station on the Hakodate Main Line. The population of Sunagawa peaked 
at 44,443 in 1948 (by Sunagawa City). Sunagawa flourished with coal 
mines in the Yuubari mountains. #1 #2
	Distinctive industries in Sunagawa are manufacturing, power 
generation, commerce, and sightseeing. STATISTICS OF AGRICULTURE = 
paddy rice 474 hectares in 2015, buckwheat 275, onions 116, pasture 87, 
fodder 77, tomatoes 20, orchards 11, flowers 7, cucumbers 2, 
	(continued) --> adzuki beans 1, radishes 1, Chinese cabbage 1, 
cabbage 1, other vegetables 9, and other grains 3; dairy cows 363 head 
in 2015.
	Factories in Sunagawa produce souvenirs from Hokkaidou; (1) 
Hori makes Yuubari Melon Pure Jelly, (2) Kitakarou makes Kaitaku-okaki 
(pioneers' rice cracker), and (3) Somes Saddle makes harness and bags. 
Also, Mitsui Chemical produces adhesives and ammonia water. Ouji 
Cornstarch, which is a company of Mitsui group, produces starch.
	As for power generation, Sunagawa Thermal Power Plant is 
operated by Hokkaidou Electric Power Company (Hokuden). The chimneys 
stand out from the Hokkaidou Expressway. There is another thermal 
power plant in neighboring Naie Town. #3
	Small local shops are open along National Route 12 in Sunagawa. 
In neighboring Takikawa City, small local shops closed. Considering its 
population, Sunagawa has many confectionery shops. ``Sunagawa Sweet 
Road'' is a project for revitalizing the city. In the past, coal miners 
bought a lot of sweets.
	The country sign (gateway sign) shows Sunagawa Reservoir and 
Hokkaidou Children's Country (Hokkaidou-kodomo-no-kuni). The reservoir 
is located downstream of the Panke-utashinai River. It is used for 
yachting, canoeing, and fishing. It used to be an ox-bow lake of the 
Ishikari River.
	Hokkaidou Children's Country is a recreational facility. It is 
connected to a service area on Hokkaidou Expressway. There are 
a pyramid, Torre di Pisa, and Lighthouse of Pharos.

#1  Upstream of Sunagawa are Utashinai City and Kami-sunagawa Town. In

#2  Sunagawa is the hometown of a writer who published a short tale 
``Ippai-no-kakesoba (One bowl of simple buckwheat noodle).'' The story
is like this;
	``Every year at the end of the year, a mother and two children 
came to a soba (buckwheat noodle) shop in Sapporo. (Japanese people eat 
buckwheat noodle at the end of the year.)
	The mother ordered one bowl of simple soba. (Nowadays, people 
order soba with tempura [deep-fried seafood and vegetables] rather than 
simple one [kake-soba].)
	The three people shared one bowl of soba. The shop owner knew 
she was poor. He served a large helping for the same price.
	Twenty years later, the two children grew up. They and their 
mother visited the same soba shop. They ordered three bowls of soba.'' 
(See Hidaka Town in Hidaka sub-prefecture for child-rearing.)
 
#3  There are thermal power plants in Sunagawa City and Naie Town.
They are inland thermal power plants. (Generally, thermal power plants 
in Japan are located along the coast.) They use coal from the Yuubari 
Mountains and cooling water from rivers. 
	Almost all underground coal mines were closed, however, open-
pit small coal mines still open. They are located in Ashibetsu City, 
Utashinai City, Mikasa City, Bibai City in Sorachi sub-prefecture, and 
Obira Town in Rumoi sub-prefecture. (The last underground coal mine in 
the country is in operation in Kushiro City.)
 	The coal from open-pit mines is used mainly in the inland 
thermal power plants and paper mills. In the 2000s, due to the rise of 
oil prices, the output of open-pit coal mines increased. 
	In the USA and Canada, shale gas and oil sands mining 
increased at the time. The energy efficiency of coal changes in 
relation to oil prices (by T. Nihei 2000. Energy efficiency of crop 
production in Japan, 1970-1990. ``Geographical Review of Japan'' 
http://www.researchgate.net/publication/272277534).
	Once, I visited the Coal Museum in Yuubari City and thought, 
``What was the energy efficiency of coal when people used pickaxes?'' 
Once, professor Hiroshi Hata (the director of Hokkaidou University 
Ranch) said, ``I wonder what the energy efficiency of the bird is 
like?'' At the time, we were on an excursion and saw a woodpecker.
	* * *
	In the past, high-quality coal from underground shafts were 
used for home stoves. Even though high-quality coal was used, stoves 
emitted smog. Children in Sapporo did not go out much during the 
winter.
	After an energy revolution, home heating changed from coal to
oil. It is changing to electricity now. The demand of electricity peaks 
in winter in Hokkaidou. It peaks in summer in Tokyo.

	In 1897 (Meiji 30), immigrants from Uzura Village in Fukui

	The town hall of Kami-sunagawa is located along the valley of 
the Panke-utashinai River. There is no national highway or railway. In

	Distinctive industry in Kami-sunagawa is manufacturing,

Manufacturing factories produce cutlery, semiconductors, and food. The

materials for shiitake mushroom cultivation such as bulk substrates 
(``kin-shou'') and wood logs (``hoda-gui'').

	On the former site of Mitsui Sunagawa Coal Mine, a zero gravity 
experiment facility was established. Experiments were conducted using a 
shaft (``tate-kou'') with a drop of 710 meters. The altitude of the 
coal mine was 120 meters. The facility was operated between 1991 and 
2003.

Utashinai [u-ta-shi-na-i] [\u6B4C\u5FD7\u5185] is a city in Sorachi 
sub-prefecture, central Hokkaidou (``dou-ou''). It is a small city in 
the Yuubari Mountains. It is located east of Sunagawa City. The Penke-
utashinai River and the Panke-utashinai River originate in Utashinai.
	The name ``Utashinai'' comes from ``ota-us-nay'' in Ainu 
language. It means ``sand, put, river.'' It signified the rivers of 
Penke-utashinai and Panke-utashinai. ``Penke'' means ``up,' and 
``Panke'' means ``down'' in Ainu language.
	In 1897 (Meiji 30), an administrative office (``kochou-
yakuba'') of Utashinai village was established. It was separated from 
Nae village (now Sunagawa City). The administrative office of Utashinai 
village also covered Furano village (now Kami-furano Town, Naka-furano 
Town and Furano City in Kamikawa sub-prefecture).
	In 1900 (Meiji 33), Ashibetsu village was separated. In 1909 
(Meiji 39), Utashinai Village (2nd-class municipality) was established. 
In 1919 (Taishou 8), Utashinai Village (1st-class municipality) was 
established. In 1922 (Taishou 11), Akabira Village was separated. (See 
the note of Nanporo Town for 1st-class and 2nd-class municipalities.)
	In 1940 (Shouwa 15), Utashinai Town was established. In 1949 
(Shouwa 24), a part of the area was separated and became a part of 
Kami-sunagawa Town. In 1958 (Shouwa 33), Utashinai City was 
established.
	The city hall of Utashinai is located along the Penke-utashinai 
River. In the past, the Utashinai Line connected Utashinai and 
Sunagawa. Utashinai was developed as a city of coal mining. Large coal 
mines were run by Sumitomo and Hokutan (Hokkaidou Colliery and 
Steamship).
	The populaton peaked at 46,171 in 1948 (by Utashinai City). As 
the main energy source changed from coal to oil, large coal mines in 
Utashinai closed from the 1950s to the 1990s. Today, Utashinai is the 
least popuated city in the country. (See Yuubari City in Sorachi sub-
prefecture for coal mining.) #1 #2 #3 #4 #5 #6
	Distinctive industries in Utashinai are sightseeing and coal 
mining. STATISTICS OF AGRICULTURE = orchards 6 hectares (grapes for 
wine) in 2015 and paddy rice - (zero); dairy cows - (zero) head in 
2015.
	As for sightseeing, there are Kamoidake International Ski 
Resort and Tyrolean Hot Spring. The city touts itself as ``The smallest 
city in the country.''
	The Kanashibetsu Romanza (movie theater) is a filming location 
for the 1970s film ``Shiawase-no-kiiroi-hankachi (The Yellow 
Handkerchief)'' and the 1980s TV drama ``Kinou, Kanashibetsu-de 
(Yesterday in Kanashibetsu).'' #7
	As for coal mining, although under-ground mines ended, an open-
pit coal mine continues. (See Sunagawa City in Sorachi sub-prefecture 
for open-pit coal mining.)

Uryuu [u-ryu-u] [\u96E8\u7ADC] is a town in Sorachi sub-prefecture, 
central Hokkaidou (``dou-ou''). The area stretches west of the Uryuu 
River and the Ishikari River. The western part of the town is the 
Mashike Mountains. Mt. Shokanbetsu (1,492 meters) rises.  
	The name ``Uryuu'' comes from ``urir-o-pet'' in Ainu language. 
It means ``cormorant, many, river.'' Cormorant is called ``u'' in 
Japanese. It signified the Uryuu River.
	In 1889 (Meiji 22), Uryuu Farm was established. It was a huge 
farm. It occupied 49,500 hecaters in the north of the Ishikari Basin. 
The area covered what is now Uryuu Town, Hokuryuu Town, Moseushi Town, 
Numata Town, and part of Fukagawa City. It was an aristocratic farm 
(peerage cooperative farm, ``kazoku-noujou [\u83EF\u65CF\u8FB2
\u5834]'' in Japanese). #1 #2
	In 1897 (Meiji 30), an administrative office (``kochou-
yakuba'') of Uryuu village was established. In 1915 (Taishou 4), Uryuu 
Village (2nd-class municipality) was established. In 1961 (Shouwa 36), 
Uryuu Town was established. (See the note of Nanporo Town for 2nd-class 
municipality.)
	The town hall of Uryuu is located near the confluence of the 
Uryuu River and the Ishikari River. National Route 275 runs. In the 
past, the Sasshou Line ran. #3
	Distinctive industries in Uryuu are paddy rice cultivation and 
sightseeing. STATISTICS OF AGRICULTURE = paddy rice 2,362 hectares in 
2015, buckwheat 334, wheat 108, beans (soybeans 29, adzuki beans 7), 
	(continued) --> melons 7, onions 2, watermelons 2, flowers 2, 
fodder 1, green onions 1, broccoli 1, tomatoes 1, and other vegetables 
12+X (concealed); dairy cow - (zero) head in 2015.
	Uryuu rice is delicious. It is cultivated with abundant 
meltwater from Mt. Shokanbetsu. As for sightseeing, Uryuu-numa Marsh is 
located at the slope of Mt. Shokanbetsu. Tourists enjoy flowers. In a 
historical park, an old building of Hachisuka Farm (the former Uryuu 
Farm) is preserved. There are large red pine trees in the park. #4 #5 
#6 #7 #8

#1  Uryuu Farm was established under the Land Grant Act in the Meiji 
period. The act was the so-called Large-land Disposal Law (daitochi-
shobun-hou).
	The owners of the farm were; (1) Sanjou Sanetomi, a court noble 
(``kugue'') in Kyouto, (2) Mochiaki Hachisuka, a duke (``kou-shaku'') 
and the former lord of the Tokushima Domain, and (3) Yukisue Kikutei, a 
court noble in Kyouto. (The place name ``Kikusui'' in Sapporo City 
is related to Yukisue Kikutei.)
	The investors were; (1) Toda Yasuhiro, a viscount (``shi-
shaku'') and the son of the lord of the Matsumoto Domain, (2) Ootani 
Kouei, an earl (``haku-shaku'') and the head priest of Higashi-honganji 
Temple, and (3) Okitomo Akimoto, a viscount and the former senior 
retainer (``karou'') of the Utsunomiya Domain (by ``Uryuu-chou-shi 
[Uryuu Town history] published in 1969.)
	They intended to practice Western-style agriculture. 
Development started in 1890 (Meiji 23). In the year, prisoners from 
Kabato Prison (Kabato-shuuchi-kan) cleared 125 hectares. They cut 
trees, dug ditches, constructed bridges and buildings, and cultivated 
crops.
	The arable land in the first year was 105 hectares. The crops 
were corn (38 hectares), rye (19), soybeans (18), adzuki beans (4), 
sugar beets, vegetables, potatoes, and buckwheat.
 	Roads that connected Kabato (now Tsukigata), Fukagawa, Mashike 
were constructed by prisoners. Immigrants moved to Uryuu Farm as tenant 
farmers.
	In the early days of development, farmers also sold forestry 
products such as timbers, matchsticks, and railroad sleepers. Those 
were exported to Naichi (Honshuu island) and the Chinese continent.
	The office of Uryuu Farm was located at the current bus stop 
``Kyuu-shigai (old town).'' It is near the Ishikari River. (See Niki 
Town in Shiribeshi sub-prefecture for large farms in the Meiji period.)
 	In the Meiji period, paddle-wheel steamers moved on the 
Ishikari River. The main river ports were Ishikari, Ebetsu, and 
Sorachi-buto (now Takikawa). (See Takikawa City in Sorachi sub-
prefecture for paddle-wheel steamers.)
	Farmers who worked for Uryuu Farm boarded paddle-wheel steamers 
and headed to Takikawa. From Takikawa, they crossed the Ishikari River 
by boat, followed a path surrounded by grass, and entered Uryuu Farm.

#2  In 1891 (Meiji 24), Sanetomi Sanjou died of influenza at the age of 
53. It was during Russian influenza pandemic. He was the most 
influential owner of Uryuu Farm. In 1893 (Meiji 26), Uryuu Farm was 
dismantled.
	In Uryuu Town, three farms were established on the site of 
Uryuu Farm; (1) Hachisuka Farm, which had 3,164 hectares of flat land 
and 3,000 hectares of mountains, (2) Toda Farm, which had 1,231 
hectares of flat land, and (3) Machimura Farm, which had 50 hectares of 
arable land and 30 hectares of pasture.
	(The average cultivated land per farm is 23 hectares in 
Hokkaidou and 1.6 hectares for the rest of Japan; according to 2010 
Census of Agriculture.)
	In Hachsuka Farm, in 1893 (Meiji 26), tenant farmers cultivated 
adzuki beans (70 hectares), corn (21), wheat (21), oats (16), and 
pasture (11). The cumulative revenue of Hachisuka Farm was in the red 
for 11 years (from 1893 to 1903).
	Hachisuka Farm and Toda Farm were owned by absentee landlords, 
while Machimura Farm was managed by Kin-ya Machimura, who was the 
former manager of Uryuu Farm. Kin-ya Machimura started dairy farming in 
Machimura Farm.
	The three farms gradually opened up their land to tenant 
farmers. The opportunity arose from disputes between landlord and 
tenant (``kosaku-sougui'') that continued from 1920 to 1932. The Hokkai 
Times reported, ``Peasants uprising. 150 tenant farmers stormed 
Hachisuka Farm office. The situation is not easy.''
	In 1917, Hirotaka Machimura, who was a son of Kin-ya Machimura, 
opened Machimura Farm in Tarukawa (now Ishikari City). In 1928, the 
farm moved from Tarukawa to Tsuishikairi (now Ebetsu City). In 1944, 
Machimura Farm in Uryuu Village was disbanded. (See Ebetsu City in 
Ishikari sub-prefecture for Machimura Farm.)
	After World War II, Hachisuka Farm and Toda Farm were disbanded 
due to post-war agrarian reform (``nouchi-kaihou'') led by Douglas 
MacArthur and the Allied Powers. (See Niki Town in Shiribeshi sub-
prefecture for large farms in the Meiji period.)

#3  There was Ishikari-oiwake Station on the Sasshou Line. A monument

``Oiwake'' is Japanese, which means ``junction.''
 	Ishikari-oiwake was a junction of old roads that led to

``Oiwake'' is also located in Abira Town, Iburi sub-prefecture, and in 
Sapporo City, Ishikari sub-prefecture.)

#5  A brief history of rice cultivation in Uryuu Town is as follows (by 
``Uryuu- chou-shi [Uryuu Town history]'' published in 1969); In 1893, 
Hachisuka Farm surveyed the possibility of irrigation from the 
Oshirarika [\u5C3E\u767D\u5229\u52A0] River. In 1904, irrigation canals 
and paddy fields were established.
	In 1910, paddy rice cultivation expanded due to the direct 
seeding (``jika-maki'') method. The main varieties at the time were 
Kurogue-wase (black hair, early ripening) and Bouzu (no hair or monk.)
	For direct seeding, heavy seeds were selected by floating them 
in salt water. Bouzu was good because it sank into the mud. Awns 
(``nogui'' or ``nogue'''] [\u8292 or \u91CE\u6BDB] of Bouzu were very 
short. (See Kita-hiroshima City in Ishikari sub-prefecture for Akague 
[red hair].)
	In the Taishou period (from 1913 to 1926), Uryuu was known as 
``Uryuu of Rice.'' However, poor harvests continued; In 1912, crop 
failure by low temperatures. In 1913, crop failure. In 1926, crop 
failure by unfavorable weather after spring. 
	(continued) --> In 1928, crop failure by hailstorm. In 1931, 
severe crop failure by low temperatures after spring. In 1932, severe 
crop failure by flooding. Farmers had to purchase government-disposed 
rice (``seifu-haraisague-mai''). In 1934, crop failure by low 
temperatures in August.
	(continued) --> In 1938, crop failure by drought. In 1940, 
crop failure by rice blast (``imochi-byou''). In 1941, crop failure by 
low temperatures. In 1943, crop failure by drought. In 1945, crop 
failure by unfavorable climate. In 1954, crop failure by typhoon.
	(continued) -->  In 1956, crop failure by low temperatures in 
summer. From 1964 to 1966, crop failures by low temperatures. 
	* * *
	Tenant farmers in the large Hachisuka Farm and Toda Farm 
cultivated not only paddy rice but also foxtail millet (``awa'') and 
glutinous proso millet (``ina-kibi'') for food.
	In 1968, the production of rice in Uryuu exceeded 200,000 hyou 
(12,000 tons). It was at the same level as current production. In the 
year, farmers established an organization to produce high-quality rice. 
They studied how to change from quantity to quality.
	 In 1985, the rate of 1st-class rice (``ittou-mai'') exceeded 
50 percent. In 1988, Kirara 397 became the recommended variety. In 
1989, the rate of 1st-class rice exceeded 70 percent.
	Today, the rate of 1st-class rice exceeds 90 percent. ``1st-
class rice'' is judged by the appearance of rice before polishing. 
``Special A,'' which was mentioned in Note 4 above, is judged by the 
taste of the cooked rice.
 
#6  These are delicious rice varieties in Hokkadou; (1) Kirara 397, 
cultivated from the early 1990s to the present, (2) Hoshino-yume, 
cultivated from the late 1990s to the early 2000s, (3) Nanatsu-boshi, 
cultivated from the early 2000s to the present, and (4) Yume-pirika, 
cultivated from the 2010s to the present.
	In the past, Hokkaidou rice was called ``neko-matagui (cat, 
step over).'' It means, ``such a tasteless rice that even cats would 
not eat.'' (See Utashinai City in Sorachi sub-prefecture for cat rice.)
	Kirara 397 changed the situation. It is delicious, whereas it 
is resistant to cold climate and rice blast. It can be cultivated 
even in peatland.
	Kirara 397 is still popular. It is sold mainly for commerce. 
Commerce rice is used in restaurants and lunch box shops. At first,

	Nanatsu-boshi became popular after the 2000s. It is sold for

	Rice for home consumption is more expensive than rice for 
commerce. It is sold at super markets. Rice for commerce includes 
imported rice. It is not sold in super markets.
	(See Pippu Town in Kamikawa sub-prefecture for Kirara 397 and

#7  As for Uryuu-numa Marsh, tourists enjoy seeing Mizu-bashou (skunk 
cabbage) in June, Ezo-kanzou (Hokkaidou daylily) in July, and Tachi-

	In Japan, Mizu-bashou in Oze Marsh in Honshuu island is 
famous. The altitude of Oze Marsh is 1,400 meters. Uryuu-numa Marsh,

	In Hokaidou, Mizu-bashou grows from zero meters altitude. In 
the case of Hokkaidou University, we can see the large leaves of Mizu-
bashou. They grow in the Sakushu-kotoni River that flows along Oono

	There is a famous Japanese song ``Natsu-no-omoide (Memory of 
summer).'' Many Japanese people think that Mizu-bashou blooms in 
summer, because the song includes the lyrics ``flowers of Mizu-bashou 
bloom... far away Oze.'' However, Mizu-bashou blooms in early spring.
	 In Sapporo, Mizu-bashou blooms in early May. It is the same 
time as the cherry blossom. Although the English name of Mizu-bashou is 
``skunk cabbage,'' Japanese people have good images of the plant 
because of the song.

 	In Japan, there are three good luck trees. They are pine, plum,

``ume (plum)'' signifies ``ume (birth).'' Bamboo signifies growing 
straight.

#8  Jinguisu-kan is grilled mutton. Matsuo Jinguisu-kan Co. sells 
seasoned mutton for barbecue. The head office is located in Takikawa 
City. The mutton is seasoned with a sweet sauce.
	There was Takikawa Sheep Ranch from 1906 to 2010. It was run by 
the Ministry of Agriculture. In the early 20th century, the government 
aimed to become self-sufficient in wool. Wool was used by the army, 
police officers, and railroad workers. The government launched the 
One Million Sheep Project.
	Japanese people started eating mutton. They did not like the 
smell of mutton. The governmental sheep ranch developed a sauce for 
grilled mutton. It was a sweet sauce. Raw mutton was dipped in the 
sauce and grilled. The sauce is the origin of Matsuo Jinguisu-kan.
	Grilled mutton was named ``Jinguisu-kan'' in the 1930s by a 
railroad officer, who worked for South Manchuria Railroad. Manchuria 
was located in what is now north China. Local people ate mutton hotpot. 
It was brought to Japan and named Jinguisu-kan after the Mongolian 
emperor Genghis Khan.
	The railroad officer was Tokuzou Komai, who graduated from 
Collage of Agriculture, Tohoku Imperial University (now Hokkaidou 
University). Before World War II, Japan occupied Manchuria. (After the 
war, Japan was occupied until 1952. [Bonin islands was occupied until 
1968 and Okinawa island was occupied until 1972].)
	Today, in Hokkaidou University, students and teachers enjoy 
Jin-pa, which is an abbreviation of Jinguisu-kan party. When I first 
heard the word, I imagined something like a dance party.
	Today, nearly 100 percent of the mutton consumed in Japan is 
imported from Australia and New Zealand. (See Toyohira Ward in Sapporo 
City, Ishikari sub-prefecture, and Shibetsu City in Kamikawa sub-
prefecture] for sheep.)
	As for sweet sauce, fish grilled in a sweet sauce is called 
``teri-yaki.'' McDonald's sells teri-yaki burgers. I think ``teri-
yaki'' is salty for the elderly people (like me). Westerners eat a lot 
of meat. The grilled meat is seasoned with a little salt (Nihei lab 
2025b).

#1  As for the name``Irakusa (hairy nettle),'' it is a plant that

soil.

Fukagawa [fu-ka-ga-wa] [\u6DF1\u5DDD] is a city in Sorachi sub-

colonies (Tonden-heison) were established. They were Minami-ichiyan, 
Kita-iciyan, and Osamunai. Minami-ichiyan was located in the current

Fukagawa Station and Rumoi Station. In 1915 (Taishou 4), Tadoshi 
[\u591A\u5EA6 \u5FD7] Village (2nd-class municipality) was established. 
It was separated from Ichiyan Village.
	In 1918 (Taishou 7), Fukagawa Town (1st-class municipality) was

him about malaria in Fukagawa, Hokkadou. He did not believed it, 
saying; ``There is no malaria in cold regions.'' Actually, malaria 
spread not only in Fukagawa but also in the Tokachi Plains. (See

	The soft drink ``Tonica [T\u00F4nica]'' that includes quinine

memories of infectious diseases and leeches from paddies. Their culture

heard `Woooo, Woooo,' a terrifying, earth-shattering roar of a bear. 
When I went outside, I hit a tin can. In a year, the bear ate 3-tan (30 
ares) of toukibi (corn). The bear ate it completely.''
	(3) ``I was a samurai (``shi-zoku'') and was a complete novice 
in agriculture. My grandfather fell ill two months after settling here. 
He died two months after becoming ill. I was the head of the household 
and had to go to military training every day. My wife did most of the 
clearing work alone.''
	(4) ``I was a child of Tonden-hei (military settler) family. 
When I became independent and had a house along Ichijou-douri (1-jou 
street), I was given `itto' (1-to) of rice as a celebration. (`Itto' is 
15 kg in the case of rice.) I consumed the `itto' of rice for one year.
	The staple foods were `ina-kibi' (glutinous proso millet), 
wheat, and barley. If someone bought `ippyou' (1-hyou, 60 kg) of rice, 
everybody's eyes widened.
	In the military settlers' colony, the rice allowance for a 
family was limited to five people, therefore, the life of a large 
family was difficult. When they got the harvest of `gosho-imo' (white 
potatoes) in autumn, they relieved.'' (See Shihoro Town in Tokachi 
sub-prefecture for gosho-imo.)
	(5) ``I was a child of Tonden-hei (militry settler) family. I 
was often sent on errands to other families. It was tough, because I 
could not understand their languages. The families came from all over 
the country, including Shikoku, Ouu, Hokuriku, and Kyuushuu regions. 
(See Fukagawa City in Sorachi sub-prefecture for dialect.) 
	Roads were bad. The land was a swamp. Water seeped onto the 
roads. Weeds and bamboo grass grew densely. The branches of tall trees 
were so intertwined that I could not see the sky even if I looked up. 
It was home to many kinds of animals.
	Nowadays, if I call loudly, neighbors can hear me. Or, I can 
put on my sandals and can run to the neighbors.''
	(6) ``The head of the household went off to military training 
every day. Development was the duty of other family members. First, we 
cut down trees and mowed the grass around the house. Then, we collected 
the trees and grass and burned them. The flames rose high into the sky. 
The flames continued for a month.
	The size of trees were 3-shaku (90 centimeters) and 4-shaku 
(120 centimeters) in diameter. Looking back now, it was regrettable 
that we wasted those large trees. Tree stumps remained in upland fields 
and paddy fields for a long time. They hindered horse-drawn plowing.''
	(7) ``Many deer lived. When I went outside the house, I found 
many deer antlers scattered in the grass. I picked them up and used 
them as hooks of the hearth (`irori') and as clothes hangers.
	The trees around the house had many nests belonging to finches 
(`mashiko'), cuckoos, owls, and other birds. Even the adult birds could 
be caught with our hands. Collecting nests and eggs was a job for the 
children when they came home from school.
	We also caught fish. There was a lot of `ugoi (ugui, big-scaled 
redfin, dace [deisu] fish).' At the time, `ugoi' were from 1-shaku (30 
centimeters) to 2-shaku (60 centimeters). No one paid any attention to 
the tiny ``funa'' (crucian carp) that are common nowadays.''

	The Fukagawa-Rumoi Expressway passes Chippubetsu Town. It takes

disposal in an American horror movie and thought it was horrible.)

|      NUMATA      |
|                  |
|  MAP 5 - NO. 24  |
 ------------------
AREA 283 KM2 (2010), FOREST 205, CULTIVATED 38; COUNTY = URYUU [\u96E8
\u7ADC]; POP 3,612 (2010), 5,206 (1990); COUNTRY SIGN = FIREFLY.
 
Numata [nu-ma-ta] [\u6CBC\u7530] is a town in Sorachi sub-prefecture, 
central Hokkaidou. It is an inland town. The area is located from the 
northern end of the Ishikari Plain to the Mashike Mountains. The Uryuu 
River and its tributary, the Horo-nii-tachi-betsu River [\u5E4C\u65B0
\u592A\u5200\u5225], flow.
  	The name ``Numata'' is Japanese. It comes from the sir name of 
Kisaburou Numata. He established Honganji Farm in what is now Numata 
Town. He was a wealthy merchant in Otaru. (Wealthy merchant is called 
``gou-shou'' in Japanese.) #1
	In 1914 (Taishou 3), the administrative office (``kochou-
yakuba'') of Kami-hokuryuu [\u4E0A\u5317\u7ADC] was established. It was 
separated from Hokuryuu village. In 1918 (Taishou 7), Horokanai village 
was separated from Kami-hokuryuu village.
	In 1919 (Taishou 8), Kami-hokuryuu Village (2nd-class 
municipality) was established. In 1922 (Taishou 11), Numata Village was 
established (Kami-hokuryuu was renamed). (See the note of Nanporo Town 
for 1st-class and 2nd-class municipalities.) 
	In 1939 (Shouwa 14), Numata Village (1st-class municipality) 
was established. In 1947 (Shouwa 22), Numata Town was established.
	The town hall of Numata is located near the Uryuu River. The 
Rumoi Main Line and National Route 275 (Sorachi-kokudou) pass. In the 
past, Numata Station was a junction between the Rumoi Main Line and the 
Sasshou Line.
	In the past, Ebishima Station was a junction between the Rumoi 
Main Line and the Rumoi Railway. The Rumoi Railway ran along the Horo-
nii-tachi-betsu River and connected coal mines of Asano, Shouwa, and 
Tachibetsu. They were called Rumoi Coal Field (Rumoi-tanden) or Uryuu 
Coal Field (Uryuu-tanden).
	Between the 1930s and the 1960s, Numata flourished from coal 
mines. In 1969 (Shouwa 44), the last coal mine was closed. In 1971 
(Shouwa 46), the Rumoi Railway was abolished. In 1972 (Shouwa 47), the 
section of the Sasshou Line, between Shin-totsukawa and Numata, was 
abolished.
	After coal mines were closed, the Japan Ground Self-defense 
Force was invited. (See Yuubari City in Sorachi sub-prefecture for coal 
mining.)
	Distinctive industries in Numata are paddy rice cultivation and 
sightseeing. STATISTICS OF AGRICULTURE = paddy rice 2,374 hectares, 
buckwheat 641, beans (soybeans 220, adzuki beans 50), wheat 206, 
	(continued) --> sugar beets 34, pasture 33, flowers 7, tomatoes 
6, fodder 4, melons 2, orchards 1, other grains 1, other vegetables X 
(concealed); dairy cows X head in 2015.
	As for sightseeing, Ebishima Station was used as a filming 
location for the NHK drama ``Suzuran (lily of the valley).'' (NHK is 
Japan Broadcasting Corporation.) Filming sets such as a wooden station 
building and a station-master's house remain.
	Hotaru-no-sato (Village of Firefly) is located along the Horo-
nii-tachi-betsu River. Fireflys fly from late June to early August.
	Village of Firefly includes Horoshin Hot Spring, the Numata 
Fossil Museum, an auto camping site, and a lifelong learning center. 
Those are managed by Numata Town. Fossil cleaning experiences at the 
Numata Fossil Museum are popular with elementary school children. #2
 
#1  Kisaburou Numata (1834-1923) was a farmer in Toyama Prefecture. At 
the age of 49, he left his wife and children behind and moved to Otaru, 
Hokkaidou.
	He put waterwheels on the Myouken River and started rice 
milling business. (The Myouken [\u5999\u898B] River is the downstream 
of Okobachi [\u65BC\u53E4\u767A] River. It flows into the Otaru Canal.)
	In 1891 (Meiji 24), he founded Kyousei, a limited partnership 
company. He added waterwheels to the Katsunai River and to the Asari 
River. (The Katsunai River flows east from the Myouken River. The 
Katsunai River flows into the Central Pier of Otaru. The Asari River 
flows east from the Myouken River. The area was Asari Village.)
	* * * 
	Kisaburou Numata opened a branch company in Sapporo. The 
wealthy merchants (``gou-shou'') of Otaru were called ``Kome-no-Numata 
(Numata of Rice)'' and ``Mame-no-Takahashi (Takahashi of Beans).'' 
Takahashi was Naoji Takahashi (1856-1926), who exported beans from 
Hokkaidou to Europe.
	Kisaburou Numata predicted, ``As rice cultivation is spreading 
in Hokkaidou, the rice milling business, which is based on imported 
rice from Honshuu island, will dwindle.''
	He planed to start rice cultivation in Hokkaidou. In 1893 
(Meiji 26), the huge Uryuu Farm in the northern Ishikari Plain was 
abolished. (See Uryuu Town in Sorachi sub-prefecture for Uryuu Farm.)
	Numata negotiated with Count Kouei Ootani of Higashi Honganji 
Temple, who was one of the investors of Uryuu Farm. He leased over 10 
million-tsubo (3,300 hectares) of land, and opened Honganji Farm at the 
former site of Uryuu Farm.
	Numata recruited immigrants from Tonami region in Toyama 
Prefecture, which was his hometown. In 1894 (Meiji 27), immigration 
started.
	In 1912 (Meiji 45), the head office of Kyousei was constructed 
in Otaru Ward (now Otaru City). It was a brick building with a copper 
roof. It is now Otaru Music Box Hall (Otaru-orugooru-kan). (A Music box 
is called ``orgel'' in Nederlands.)

#2  In geology, Numata Town is known for the fossils of Takahashi-
hotate, which are large scallops that lived 5 million years ago. (They 
became extinct.)
	 At the Numata Fossil Museum, visitors can try cleaning 
fossils. The fossils are small ammonites and shark teeth imported from 
Morocco. Visitors can take them home after cleaning.
	Numata Fossil Museum opened in 2000. In May 2015, my son, 
Masaki Nihei, became the 15,000th visitor. In the lifelong learning 
center of Numata Town, a large totem pole is displayed. It is a gift 
from the sister city, Port Hardy.
	Port Hardy is located in the north of Vancouver Island, Canada. 
Numata Town and Port Hardy became sister cities through the mediation 
of the president of a fish processing factory in Numata Town. 
	Another sister city is Oyabe City in Tonami region, Toyama 
Prefecture. The Yotaka-andon Festival is held in Numata Town in August. 
In the festival, large lamp floats collide with each other. The 
festival originated in Oyabe City.
